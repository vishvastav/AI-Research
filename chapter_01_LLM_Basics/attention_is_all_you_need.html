Attention Is All You Need

How a 2017 paper taught computers to understand language by letting words look at each other.

arXiv:1706.03762 — the paper that built GPT, Claude, BERT, T5
The Problem · Before 2017

Reading word by word was slow and forgetful

Old models (RNN / LSTM) processed sentences one word at a time, like reading aloud. By the end of a long sentence, they had often forgotten the start.

Old way: sequential

The cat will sit on mat

Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3 → ... can't parallelize. Memory fades.

New way: parallel attention

The cat will sit on mat

All words seen at once. Each word looks at every other word.

Big idea: Throw away sequence. Let every word "pay attention" to every other word, in parallel.
Step 1 · Tokenize

Sentence becomes pieces (tokens)

Break text into small units the model can handle. Could be whole words, parts of words, or punctuation.

Input sentence

"The cat will sit on the mat"

After tokenization

The cat will sit on the mat
Each token gets a unique ID — e.g. The = 464, cat = 3797. Just integers so far.
Step 2 · Embed

Each token becomes a vector (a list of numbers)

Integer ID alone tells us nothing. The model learns to map each token to a vector that captures its meaning. Similar words land near each other.

cat → 0.21-0.830.470.12...0.55 (512 numbers)
sit → -0.050.410.62-0.31...0.18 close to "lie", "rest"
mat → 0.33-0.12-0.780.55...-0.22 close to "rug", "carpet"
Geometry of meaning: words with related meanings have similar vectors. Math can now compare them.
Step 3 · Query, Key, Value

Each word produces three different views of itself

From each word vector, three new vectors are created via learned weight matrices. Think of a library:

Q — Query

"What am I looking for?" — what this word wants to know about other words.

K — Key

"What do I offer?" — a label other words use to find this one.

V — Value

"What I actually carry" — the content this word contributes if attended to.

Library analogy

You ask a question (Q). Library catalog has tags (K). The book itself is V. Match Q to K, fetch V.

catQ catK catV | sitQ sitK sitV
Step 4 · Score

How much does "sit" care about every other word?

Take "sit"'s Query, dot-product it with every other word's Key. High number = strong match.

Raw scores for "sit" looking at each word

sitQ · TheK = 0.8 | sitQ · catK = 4.2 | sitQ · matK = 3.1 | sitQ · willK = 1.5
Reading this: "sit" cares a lot about "cat" (who sits?) and "mat" (where?). It cares less about "The".

Math: score(i, j) = Qi · Kj / √dk  — scaling by √dk keeps numbers stable.

Step 5 · Softmax

Turn raw scores into attention weights (sum to 1)

Softmax converts arbitrary numbers into percentages. Big scores get amplified. Small ones flattened.

Before softmax

The: 0.8
cat: 4.2
will: 1.5
sit: 2.0
on: 0.6
mat: 3.1

After softmax (weights)

The: 0.02
cat: 0.52 ← top focus
will: 0.04
sit: 0.07
on: 0.01
mat: 0.34 ← second focus
These weights tell "sit": 52% of your attention to "cat", 34% to "mat", ignore the rest.
Step 6 · Combine

Mix the Values using those weights

"sit" now becomes a blend: mostly cat's value, some of mat's value, tiny bit of everything else. This is its new context-aware vector.

Step 7 · Multi-Head

Eight pairs of eyes are better than one

A single attention pass captures one "view" of how words relate. Multi-head attention runs 8 (or more) separate attention computations in parallel — each free to learn a different pattern.

Head 1 · Syntax

Subject ↔ verb. "cat" ↔ "sit".

Head 2 · Location

Verb ↔ where. "sit" ↔ "mat".

Head 3 · Articles

"The" ↔ its noun.

Head 4 · Tense / aspect

"will" ↔ "sit" (future action).

Outputs from all 8 heads are concatenated and projected back. The model picks up many relationships at once, without any human telling it what to look for.
The Transformer · Stacked Blocks

Stack this 12, 24, or 96 times — that's a transformer

One attention block contextualizes once. Stacking many blocks lets the model build deeper meanings — layer 1 sees grammar, layer 12 sees abstract intent.

Encoder stack

Why It Mattered

One idea, three superpowers

1. Parallel

No more reading one token at a time. GPUs process the whole sentence at once. Training time drops from weeks to days.

2. Long-range memory

Token 1 talks directly to token 1000. No gradient fading. Models suddenly understand long documents, books, code.

3. Scales beautifully

Same architecture, more data, more parameters — and it keeps getting better. GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama: all transformers.

4. General purpose

Works on text, images (ViT), audio (Whisper), proteins (AlphaFold-3), code. Attention is universal.

"Attention Is All You Need" is the architectural blueprint of every modern LLM you use. 8 pages of math in 2017 → the foundation of GPT-5, Claude 4.7, Gemini.
